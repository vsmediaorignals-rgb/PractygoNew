What Is E-E-A-T and Why Does Google Rank Your Clinic on It?

Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness — Google's quality guidelines for medical content are strict. Here's how to build all four signals for your practice.

✍️ Practygo Team📅 Jan 5, 2025⏱️ 10 min read

If you've ever wondered why some clinic websites rank consistently well on Google despite having fewer backlinks than competitors, E-E-A-T is often the answer. Google's quality raters use these four dimensions to evaluate every health-related page — and in 2025, the signals have never mattered more.

What Is E-E-A-T?

E-E-A-T stands for Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, and Trustworthiness. It's not a direct ranking factor (there's no single "E-E-A-T score") but rather a framework Google's algorithms use to evaluate content quality, particularly for YMYL (Your Money or Your Life) topics — a category that includes all healthcare content.

  • Experience — Has the author actually experienced what they're writing about? For a doctor writing about diabetes management, lived clinical experience matters.
  • Expertise — Does the author have formal qualifications and deep knowledge in the subject area?
  • Authoritativeness — Is the author or website recognised as an authority by others in the field? (Measured partly through backlinks, citations, and mentions.)
  • Trustworthiness — Is the content accurate, honest, and transparent? Is the website secure and clearly identifying who runs it?

Why Healthcare Gets Special Scrutiny

Google explicitly states in its Search Quality Rater Guidelines that medical advice content requires the highest levels of E-E-A-T because low-quality information can directly harm people's health. A clinic website that gives incorrect information about drug interactions or symptoms isn't just bad SEO — it's dangerous. Google's algorithms reflect this.

This is why generic content farm articles and AI-written health pages without author attribution have been systematically downranked since Google's Helpful Content Update. And why a cardiologist's website with verified credentials, cited content, and real clinical experience can outrank a large health portal with more traffic but shallower authority in that specific specialty.

Building the Experience Signal

Experience is the newest addition to the framework (it was previously E-A-T). For healthcare professionals, demonstrating experience means including: the number of patients you've treated, years of clinical practice, specific case types you're experienced with, and — where appropriate and consented — real patient outcomes data. "Over 2,000 successful knee replacement surgeries in 15 years" is an experience signal. "Experienced orthopaedic surgeon" is not.

Building the Expertise Signal

Expertise is primarily demonstrated through content and credentials. Every piece of content on your website should be attributed to a named, qualified author. Your about page should list your full degree history, fellowship details, specialisation training, and any academic publications. If you write blog posts, your by-line should link to your author profile which lists these credentials.

Building the Authoritativeness Signal

Authority is the hardest to build and the most valuable when you have it. Key authority signals for Indian healthcare professionals include: being mentioned or quoted in health journalism (HealthShots, TheHealthSite, Hindustan Times Health), being listed as a faculty member or speaker at medical conferences, having hospital or university affiliations link to your profile, and earning patient-review volume on multiple platforms (Google, Practo, JustDial). Guest articles on reputable health platforms are particularly effective — each one creates an authoritative backlink and a citation.

Building the Trustworthiness Signal

Trust signals that Google and patients both value: HTTPS security (mandatory — no exceptions), clear ownership information (your name, NMC number, and clinic details on every page), a physical address that matches your Google Business Profile, a clear disclaimer on all medical content ("This is for informational purposes only — consult your physician"), and a visible privacy policy. Negative trust signals that actively hurt ranking: no SSL certificate, no author attribution, outdated contact information, and content that makes unverifiable claims.

🧠 Practygo E-E-A-T audit finding: Fewer than 8% of Indian clinic websites we've reviewed have a named author on their blog content, and under 15% display their NMC registration number. Both are simple wins with significant ranking impact.
